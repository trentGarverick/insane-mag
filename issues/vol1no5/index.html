FROM THE EDITOR'S BUNKER

Dear Reader,

There are weeks when the news arrives like a polite stream and weeks when it arrives like a fire hose held by a man who has never seen a fire, a hose, or water. This was the second kind.

In the span of a single news cycle, the President walked off a Sunday morning interview, got booed by his own fans at a basketball game, watched Congress shovel $70 billion at the border, watched a surveillance law teeter on the edge of a calendar, and watched a Maine oyster farmer absorb more opposition research than a sponge in a flood. We are not making any of this up. We checked. We were disappointed to confirm it.

Our cover this week tried to fit all of it onto one page and frankly the page filed a complaint. The central image — the President spinning on a game-show platform labeled PUBLIC OPINION — is the only honest illustration we have ever published, in the sense that the platform is, in fact, spinning, and nobody is sure which way.

We dedicate this issue to the one fixed point in a whirling universe: the off switch on your television, which still works, and which we beg you to remember exists.

Stay screened, America. They're checking everything else.

— The Editors, INSANE Magazine

    COVER STORY · THE SUNDAY MELTDOWN

    MEET THE STRESS

    PRESIDENT ANSWERS TOUGH QUESTION WITH SURPRISE EXIT

    On Sunday, June 7, the President sat for a Meet the Press interview, was asked questions, discovered that questions exist, and concluded the segment by removing his own microphone and leaving — a maneuver experts are calling 'the reverse interview.'

    It began, as these things do, with a question. The anchor — calm, prepared, armed only with publicly available facts — asked the President about election-fraud claims, about California voting, about January 6, and about the curiously named 'anti-weaponization fund.' The President, sensing a trap consisting entirely of things he had personally said, did what any cornered leader of the free world would do: he removed his own microphone and walked off the set.

    Eyewitnesses report the exit took approximately four seconds, during which the President set a new personal record for distance traveled away from a follow-up question.

    The interview had been billed as a routine sit-down. It became, instead, a kind of nature documentary: here is the apex predator of cable news, and here is what happens when you gently ask it for a source.

    Sources inside the studio say the lie-detector machine — installed purely as a prop — began smoking around the third question and printed a single strip reading 'ERROR: QUESTION DETECTED.' Technicians say the machine has since requested early retirement and a quieter assignment, possibly weather.

    At the center of it all sat the anchor, who did not flinch, did not raise her voice, and did not abandon the question merely because the subject of the question abandoned the building. She simply held the microphone forward and let the silence do the fact-checking.

    "She just sat there being correct," said one stunned producer. "It was the most aggressive thing I have ever seen a person do while sitting completely still."

    INSANE would like to formally salute the anchor for the increasingly radical act of asking a question and then waiting for the answer. In an era of meltdowns, holding your ground is a competitive sport, and she medaled.

    "I asked one follow-up. He treated it like a subpoena." — A composed professional, paraphrased

    REACTION DESK · AMERICA RESPONDS

    VIEWERS REPORT MILD CONCUSSION OF EXPECTATIONS

    A NATION SET DOWN ITS COFFEE AT THE SAME MOMENT

    We canvassed the viewing public, by which we mean we made up six representative Americans whose reactions are nonetheless emotionally accurate.

    Reaction to the walk-off was swift, bipartisan, and largely consisted of the word 'whoa' said at increasing volumes.

    "I tuned in for the weather," said Donna Pellington of Akron, Ohio. "By the time I looked up, the man was gone and the chair was still warm. I have never seen furniture so disappointed."

    "I'm a lifelong supporter and even I said out loud, to my dog, 'buddy, you have to answer the question,'" reported Carl Sledge of Bristol, Tennessee. "The dog agreed. The dog has more follow-through."

    "What got me," said Maria Castellano of Queens, New York, "is that the lady just sat there. Didn't blink. I respect that. I couldn't sit that still at the DMV and they have my whole future."

    "It was like watching someone flip the Monopoly board because they landed on Income Tax," said Reverend Bo Tweedy of Macon, Georgia. "And we were only on the second roll."

    "I rewound it eleven times," said Pat Quigley of Spokane, Washington. "Each time, he still left. Television used to have suspense."

    "I don't follow politics," admitted Tiffani Roe of Mesa, Arizona, "but I know a clip when I see one, and that, my friend, is a clip."

    SPORTS DESK · THE SEQUEL NOBODY GREENLIT

    FROM MEET THE PRESS TO MEET THE JEERS

    PRESIDENT APPEARS ON JUMBOTRON, RECEIVES BIPARTISAN BOO

    Twenty-four hours after walking off a television set, the President walked into Madison Square Garden for Game 3 of the NBA Finals and was greeted by a feature New York has spent two centuries perfecting: the boo.

    It started as cheers. It is important to be fair: there were cheers. The cheers lasted roughly as long as a New York minute, which is to say less than a minute, after which the boos arrived like weather.

    The boos came from the rafters, from the floor seats, from the luxury boxes, and — eyewitnesses insist — from at least one hot dog vendor who booed reflexively without looking up.

    INSANE's official scorecard, kept by hand for accuracy, recorded the final tally at BOOS 115, CHEERS 111, after overtime. The boos pulled ahead late in the fourth and never relinquished the lead. Cheers fouled out.

    Most remarkable was the bipartisanship. "I came here to boo the refs," said one fan, "but I am a flexible man." Another held a foam finger reading WE'RE #BOO, which sold out at the merchandise stand within minutes, leaving the actual team's jerseys untouched.

    Sports analysts noted that booing at the Garden is among the oldest and most respected local traditions, predating the franchise, the building, and possibly the city. "New Yorkers will boo a sunset if it lingers," one explained. "This is just heritage."

    DOWN-BALLOT · MAINE PRIMARY DAY

    MAINE COURSE: SCANDAL STEW

    THE OYSTER CANDIDATE GETS SHUCKED — AND SOMEHOW STAYS AFLOAT

    It is primary day in Maine, where Democrats are choosing a nominee to face Senator Susan Collins, and where the frontrunner has absorbed a quantity of opposition research normally reserved for entire administrations.

    Graham Platner is a former Marine, an oyster farmer, and the kind of populist who polls well right up until someone opens a folder. This week, several folders were opened.

    The frontrunner has faced scrutiny over explicit texts, allegations regarding his treatment of women, and a tattoo critics linked to Nazi imagery — which he has since covered up and apologized for. By the standards of a normal candidacy, any one of these would be disqualifying. By the standards of 2026, it was Tuesday.

    And yet. Despite the scandal stew simmering on every burner, Platner remained the frontrunner heading into the primary, buoyed by genuine grassroots enthusiasm and high-profile progressive backing, including Senator Bernie Sanders, who suggested the public cared more about groceries than gossip.

    "We've got a housing crisis and people can't afford healthcare," Sanders said, more or less, declining to spend his energy on the candidate's marriage.

    INSANE takes no position on the race, but we do take a position on the lobster, who has been dragging the opposition-research file up and down the dock for three days and would like overtime. "I'm a crustacean, not a forklift," the lobster did not say, because lobsters cannot speak, a fact we confirmed with the oyster, who also could not speak, leaving this entire paragraph unsourced and proud of it.

    Voters head to the polls today. The oysters remain neutral. The scandals remain, per the sign, stale but available.

    POLICY DESK · THE BIG SCREENING

    CHECKPOINT NATION

    $70 BILLION BUYS A LOT OF CONVEYOR BELTS

    House Republicans advanced a roughly $70 billion immigration-enforcement package — and turned the Capitol into the world's most expensive airport security line in the process.

    The package, which Democrats opposed, funnels enormous sums into enforcement through 2029: reports put it at roughly $38 billion for ICE, $26 billion for CBP, and $5 billion for DHS. To help visualize $70 billion, picture a money cannon. Now picture it on.

    Tucked into the fight were the add-ons that make a spending bill feel handcrafted: a ballroom fund and an 'anti-weaponization' fund of approximately $1 billion, a phrase that means whatever the reader fears most.

    "We're not building a wall, we're building a customer experience," explained a fictional spokesperson we invented to avoid quoting a real one. "Everyone gets screened. Phones, visas, pilots, the Moon. The Moon especially. We don't trust the Moon."

    At Checkpoint Nation, the lines are long, the scanners are loud, and the conveyor belt moves immigrants, oil barrels, and the occasional confused basketball with equal suspicion. A sign overhead reminds travelers to remove their shoes, their rights, and any expectation of an itemized receipt.

    Asked where the $1 billion anti-weaponization fund actually goes, officials gestured vaguely at the money cannon, which was, at that moment, weaponizing.

    SURVEILLANCE DESK · TICK TOCK

    REMOVE RIGHTS FROM POCKETS

    FISA SECTION 702 SET TO EXPIRE JUNE 12 — PROBABLY — MAYBE

    The surveillance authority known as Section 702 is set to expire June 12, and lawmakers are once again fighting over whether agencies should need a warrant to search Americans' communications swept up while targeting foreigners.

    Section 702 lets the government collect communications of foreign targets abroad. The catch — the part that keeps coming back to Congress like a houseguest who never reads the room — is that Americans' messages get swept up 'incidentally,' a word doing more heavy lifting than any other word in Washington.

    The debate, boiled down: should officials need a warrant before searching that incidentally collected pile for an American's communications? One side says yes, obviously. The other side says warrants are slow and the houseguest is already comfortable.

    With the clock ticking toward Thursday, the Privacy Express Lane is open. Travelers are asked to remove their shoes, their phones, and their reasonable expectation that 'incidentally' means 'rarely.'

    "It's a perfectly balanced system," said a fictional official we made up. "We balance your privacy against our curiosity, and curiosity has a really good lawyer."

    If Congress does nothing, the authority lapses Thursday. If Congress does something, it will likely be at 11:58 PM Wednesday, which is the constitutionally mandated deadline for all American governance.

    COURTS DESK · TOO EXPENSIVE TO EXIST

    DENIED — THE FEE, THIS TIME

    BOSTON JUDGE VOIDS $100,000 H-1B VISA CHARGE

    A federal judge in Boston ruled against the administration's $100,000 H-1B visa fee, finding it exceeded executive authority and amounted to a tax imposed without Congress — which, it turns out, is still Congress's whole job.

    The administration's idea was simple: charge $100,000 for an H-1B visa and watch the demand for skilled foreign workers politely evaporate. The flaw was also simple: a $100,000 charge that walks like a tax and quacks like a tax is, a judge ruled, a tax — and only Congress gets to impose those.

    "You cannot invent a six-figure fee to exist," the judge did not say in exactly those words, but the ruling vibed accordingly.

    The decision is a reprieve for tech firms and the engineers they employ, several of whom had begun pricing out whether it was cheaper to simply relocate the entire company to a country that lets people in for under a Tesla.

    INSANE's economics desk notes that '$100,000 to exist' was, briefly, the most honest pricing the immigration system has ever offered, in that it at least listed the price up front.
