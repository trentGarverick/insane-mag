FROM THE EDITOR'S BUNKER

Dear Reader,

Every so often a single story arrives so perfectly shaped that it becomes a parable, a diorama, a snow globe you can shake to watch an entire era swirl around inside it. This week, that snow globe was full of algae.

The Lincoln Memorial Reflecting Pool — a shallow rectangle of water whose entire job is to lie still and reflect things — was renovated for more than $14 million. It was painted 'American flag blue.' Within roughly two weeks the blue began peeling off in long rubbery flaps and the water turned the green of a forgotten aquarium. Workers arrived with shop-vacs and hydrogen peroxide. The President announced that vandals were responsible, and that a man would need to go to jail. The man, it turned out, was a 67-year-old three-time Olympic canoeist who said he touched a loose flap out of curiosity.

We did not invent any of that. We want to be very clear, because we understand the temptation to assume we did. We are a satire magazine and even we would have rejected this plot for being too on-the-nose.

The pool was supposed to reflect the monuments around it. Instead it reflected the whole project: the rushed announcement, the no-bid contract, the patriotic paint, the peeling, the blame, the bystander in handcuffs, and the promise to drain it all and start over. The pool did its one job after all. It reflected everything.

Mind the algae, America. It always comes back.

— The Editors, INSANE Magazine

    COVER STORY · THE MAIN EVENT

    THE POOL THAT REFLECTED THE PRESIDENCY

    $14 MILLION LATER, THE WATER IS THE WRONG COLOR

    It was supposed to be 'American flag blue.' Ahead of the nation's 250th anniversary, the Lincoln Memorial Reflecting Pool was renovated, repainted, and refilled. Within about two weeks, the blue began peeling off and the water turned a confident, unpatriotic green.

    The Reflecting Pool has one job. It is a shallow rectangle of water and its entire purpose, since 1922, has been to hold still and reflect the things around it. This is, by the standards of federal infrastructure, an extremely achievable goal.

    The President declared the pool 'absolutely filthy' and an 'embarrassment to our Country,' and ordered it fixed and recolored to a 'dark blue of the American Flag' ahead of the semiquincentennial. He praised the chosen material personally, calling it thick, strong, flexible, and possessed of a natural, beautiful color.

    The material survived, by most accounts, less than two weeks at the bottom of a pool before photos showed it peeling away and floating cheerfully to the surface like it had somewhere better to be.

    Then the algae came back. Algae has returned to this pool after essentially every renovation since the Coolidge administration, a fact the Interior Department itself cited, which raises the question of why anyone expected a fresh coat of paint to settle a 104-year-old argument the algae has been winning the whole time.

    The cost, originally estimated at $1.8 million, climbed past $14.65 million — roughly $4 million over even the revised estimate — with one unexplained $460,000 transaction that the federal database lists and no human being has yet explained.

    Workers arrived with shop-vacs, gallons of hydrogen peroxide, and a machine that blows ozone nanobubbles. As of this writing the pool remains green in large sections, the blue continues to peel, and the monuments around it are being reflected, faithfully, in a swamp.

    "The pool has one job. The job is reflection. It is now reflecting our entire situation back at us, which feels like overreach." — INSANE Civic Desk

    FOLLOW THE MONEY · CONTRACTS DESK

    MEET THE NO-BID POOL GUY

    FIRST FEDERAL CONTRACT, FINEST GOLF-CLUB RÉSUMÉ

    The resurfacing work went to a Virginia coatings firm whose prior credential, by multiple accounts, was doing pool work at the President's own golf club — awarded without competitive bidding, citing 'unusual and compelling urgency' around the 250th anniversary.

    There is a phrase in federal contracting — 'unusual and compelling urgency' — that lets the government skip the competitive bidding process when there's no time to lose. It is meant for emergencies: disasters, wartime, things that cannot wait.

    It was used here for the color of a pool.

    The contract to waterproof and repaint went to Atlantic Industrial Coatings, a Virginia firm reportedly making its first-ever federal contract, whose relevant experience was said to include pool work at the President's Sterling golf club. There was, briefly, some confusion about who the contractor even was, after the President mentioned speaking to 'a man he knew' about the job and then walked that back.

    Reporting also pointed to a separate $1.7 million no-bid filtration contract for 'nanobubble' technology, awarded to a firm connected to a donor-linked trust. The phrase 'donor-linked trust' is doing the same heavy lifting as 'incidentally' did last issue. Washington runs on phrases that mean more the longer you stare at them.

    INSANE's contracts desk has reviewed the urgency exemption and found it compelling, in that we are compelled to ask what the emergency was. The pool had been still for a century. It could, arguably, have waited for a second bid.

    JUSTICE DESK · THE VANDAL MENACE

    VANDALS! SAD! VERY SAD!

    OLYMPIAN, 67, DETAINED FIVE HOURS FOR TOUCHING A FLAP

    When the blue began peeling, the President announced that vandals were responsible — providing no evidence — and that 'many additional people' had been arrested for serious crimes carrying years in jail. One of the arrested was a 67-year-old three-time Olympic canoeist who says he touched a loose piece out of curiosity.

    The peeling was a problem. Problems require culprits. And so, overnight and without evidence, the culprit was identified: vandals. Shadowy, monument-destroying vandals, who had somehow sabotaged a coating that several engineers suspected of being entirely capable of peeling on its own.

    "These are very serious crimes having to do with the destruction of National Monuments. Years in jail," the President wrote, of the pool surface.

    The most prominent person swept up was David Hearn, 67, a three-time U.S. Olympic canoeist, who told reporters he touched a partially detached flap of blue material out of curiosity and was promptly detained for about five hours.

    "I didn't vandalize anything," Hearn said. "I didn't destroy or break or peel anything. By the time I realized what was going on, I was being put in handcuffs."

    He is due in court on July 9, where the prosecution will face the challenge of explaining how a flap that was already floating got blamed on a man who, by his account, simply confirmed with one finger that it was, in fact, floating.

    INSANE would like to note for the record that touching a thing to see if it is loose is the single most human act there is, and that if curiosity is now a federal monument crime, the entire species should turn itself in.

    "I just touched it!" — David Hearn, 67, three-time Olympian, alleged flap-toucher

    TECHNOLOGY DESK · THE SOLUTION

    BEHOLD THE NANOBUBBLE 3000

    KILLS ALGAE — CREATES EXCUSES

    Faced with a pool that had turned green, officials deployed hydrogen peroxide, shop-vacs, and what the Interior Department described as 'advanced nanobubbler technology.' The algae remained, as of press time, deeply unbothered.

    When water turns green, modern government has options. You can drain it. You can treat it. Or you can deploy a machine with a number in its name and let the number do the reassuring.

    The Nanobubble approach involves pumping tiny ozone bubbles into the water to combat algae — a real technology, deployed here at real expense, for a roughly $1.7 million no-bid contract. Workers also poured jugs of hydrogen peroxide directly into the pool, which an Interior spokesperson assured the public has 'no harmful side effects to marine life,' a sentence that assumes the marine life had been consulted.

    The Interior Department further explained that algae has 'plagued every Lincoln Reflecting Pool reopening since 1922,' which is true, and which is also the strongest possible argument that the algae is not vandalism but simply the pool's natural personality.

    In perhaps the week's most remarkable government communication, Interior compared the dead algae being vacuumed off the pool floor to 'the destroyed Iranian Navy resting on the bottom of the Persian Gulf.' INSANE has read that sentence eleven times and can confirm it does not improve.

    The nanobubbles, meanwhile, continue to rise, pop, and — if you watch closely — spell out the official position.

    EXPECT DELAYS, MISSILES, TOLL TALK & LIGHT HONKING

    Good morning, commuters. Traffic through the world's most important oil chokepoint is moving — despite renewed talk of closure, which the U.S. military denied, saying tankers continued through the strait.

    And now to our traffic correspondent, who is hovering over the Strait of Hormuz, where conditions this morning are described as 'tense but flowing.'

    Reports of a full closure have been denied by the U.S. military, which noted that vessels continued moving through the strait. So if you're commuting by supertanker today, you're cleared to proceed, though you may experience residual anxiety in the left lane.

    Expect the usual: bumper-to-bumper tankers, periodic toll-talk, the occasional missile in the carpool lane, and light honking. One vessel was observed displaying 'PLEASE TAKE A NUMBER,' which our correspondent notes is either a joke or the single most efficient idea the region has produced in years.

    Oil prices, as always, ride the situation like a roller coaster with no posted height requirement. Analysts advise commuters to remain calm, keep two tanker-lengths of distance, and remember that the strait, like the Reflecting Pool, has been a problem for a very long time and will likely be a problem for a very long time more.

    ARCTIC DESK · NORTHERN MANEUVERS

    GREENLAND POLAR BEAR MILITIA BEGINS DRILLS

    NOT FOR SALE — BUT IGLOOS AVAILABLE TO RENT

    Amid renewed pressure to acquire or control Greenland — which has strained relations with European NATO allies — the island's most committed residents have organized. They are large. They are cold-adapted. They are not interested in your offer.

    Greenland is not for sale. This has been stated repeatedly, by Greenland, in a tone that suggests it would prefer not to keep saying it. The renewed push to acquire or control the island has unsettled European NATO members and delighted exactly one constituency: the polar bears, who have discovered the concept of leverage.

    INSANE's Arctic bureau reports that a volunteer polar bear militia has begun holding weekend drills, marching in loose formation across the ice with wooden rifles and an admirable sense of purpose. Their stated position, per a sign held by their apparent leader, is: NOT FOR SALE, BUT WE DO RENT IGLOOS.

    This is, our correspondent notes, the most reasonable diplomatic posture currently on offer anywhere in the Arctic: a firm no on the sovereignty question, paired with a flexible yes on short-term lodging.

    Asked for comment, the bears growled, which our translator rendered as 'first and last month's rent, no parties, and the security deposit covers seal damage only.'

    NATO officials, reached for comment, asked to be left out of it.

    PROTOCOL DESK · GIFTS & GRATITUDE

    TOTALLY NORMAL GIFT

    JUST A REGULAR-SIZED JUMBO JET WITH A BOW ON IT

    Nothing to see here, says the banner on the enormous gifted aircraft. INSANE files this under our recurring department, 'Things Being Described As Normal At High Volume.'

    There is a useful rule in life: the more loudly something insists it is normal, the more questions it tends to generate. A luxury jumbo jet, wrapped in a bow and towing a banner reading TOTALLY NORMAL GIFT, is a near-perfect demonstration of this rule in flight.

    INSANE takes no position on any specific aircraft. We simply observe that ordinary gifts — socks, a casserole, a nice card — do not typically require aerial signage clarifying their ordinariness.

    Our protocol desk reviewed the relevant ethics guidance and found it, like the pool, in the process of being drained.

    We wish the jet well, wherever it lands, and whoever ends up holding the bow.
