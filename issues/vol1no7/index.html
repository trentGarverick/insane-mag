FROM THE EDITOR'S BUNKER

Dear Reflective Reader,

We stood at the edge of the Lincoln Memorial Reflecting Pool this week, gazed into its $16-million algae-green surface, and asked the eternal question of our republic: who did this? The pool, performing the one job a reflecting pool has ever had, showed us the answer. We are not allowed to print the answer. The answer has retained counsel.

It has been a banner 36 hours. The Senate voted 50–48 to tell the President to stop a war — the first time in the 50-year history of the law that both chambers have agreed on anything this strongly, and also, we are told, a vote with roughly the legal force of a strongly-worded Yelp review. The President called the four Republicans who crossed over “Losers.” The Republicans called it “a briefing they never got.” The war continued to cost $87.6 billion regardless, which the White House would now like Congress to please pay for, along with some Ebola money and $11.1 billion for farmers, all in the same sandbag.

Meanwhile Iran agreed, according to the President, to “highest level” nuclear inspections — “Infinity!!!” Iran, according to Iran, agreed to no such thing. Both statements were issued on the same afternoon, from the same planet, about the same uranium. We have printed both, side by side, in a feature we are calling “The Accord That Disagrees With Itself.”

And in a rare outbreak of Congress doing its literal job, both chambers passed the largest housing bill in a generation — 85–5 and 358–32 — only for the President to cancel the signing ceremony two hours before it began, chaining his signature to a separate voter-ID bill like a houseboat lashed to a buoy. The housing bill becomes law in ten days whether he signs it or not, which is the legislative equivalent of refusing to blow out a candle that is going to go out anyway.

Down in New York, a subway car full of Mamdani-endorsed socialists crashed directly into the Democratic establishment and took two incumbents with it. We cover that too. We cover everything. We are exhausted. The pool is still green.

Drain responsibly. Happy 250th. BYO algae.

— The Editors, dabbing at their waders

P.S. The duck on the cover wearing the snorkel is a credentialed IAEA inspector. He has clearance for the shallow end only. The deep end is “pending a final deal and sanctions relief.”

THE POOL THAT REFLECTS EVERYTHING

The Lincoln Memorial Reflecting Pool was renovated for the nation's 250th birthday at a cost of $16 million, and in return for that sum the American people received a pool that is, at press time, the color of a smoothie made entirely of regret. The water is algae-green. The new paint — a shade officially logged as “American Flag Blue” — is peeling into the water in patriotic flakes. Ducklings paddle through it wearing what our photographer swears are tiny safety goggles.

Democrats are demanding investigations. The President has blamed vandals, citing a claimed “350-foot gash” in the pool liner, for which — and we want to be precise here — no public evidence has yet been produced. The sign at the water's edge reads “350-FOOT GASH — EVIDENCE PENDING,” which is also, increasingly, the official motto of the Republic.

“Someone did this,” a source close to the pool told us, gesturing at the pool, which was at that moment reflecting the source perfectly. “A bad someone. A vandal someone. Definitely not anyone who signed the invoice.” When we pointed out that the reflection appeared to be pointing back at the source, the source said the reflection was “fake news water” and left.

Contractors and the White House defend the work, noting that the pool is, technically, still a pool, and that algae is a “naturally occurring patriot” that “loves this country.” The National Park Service was unavailable, having last been seen wading out toward the center holding a net and weeping softly into it.

INSANE reached out to the algae for comment. The algae, which has been winning continuously since the pool opened in 1922, declined, citing momentum.

The pool was asked who did this. Being a reflecting pool, it answered. That was the whole problem.

THE ACCORD THAT DISAGREES WITH ITSELF

In one of the great achievements of modern diplomacy, the United States and Iran this week reached an accord that the two parties describe completely differently while standing in the same room. The President holds up a document with two columns. The left column is headed WHAT TRUMP SAID. The right column is headed WHAT IRAN SAYS. The columns do not match. They were never going to match. Matching was not in the framework.

Per the President, Iran agreed to “highest level” nuclear inspections, “anywhere, anytime,” “Infinity!!!” — a unit of inspection that, our science desk confirms, does not come with batteries and cannot be independently verified, much like infinity itself.

Per Iran, Iran has agreed to no “Infinity.” Iranian officials say inspectors may return under an interim framework, but that access to the facilities the U.S. recently bombed — the interesting ones, with the underwater uranium barrels our cover artist so lovingly rendered — depends on a final deal and on sanctions relief. “Not so fast,” the right column reads. “We will decide.”

The IAEA, caught in the middle wearing a snorkel, confirms that some inspections will happen under the interim framework, and that the rest will happen approximately when everyone agrees on what they agreed to. Our IAEA inspector duck has been cleared for the shallow end. He reports the shallow end is fine. The deep end remains “pending.”

We ran both columns past a neutral arbitrator. The arbitrator read the left column, read the right column, read them again, and is now also a reflecting pool.

“Infinity!!!” said the left column. “We will decide,” said the right column. They are the same document. This is the document.

WAR POWERS: NOW AVAILABLE IN SYMBOLIC SIZE

For the first time in the 50-year life of the War Powers law, both chambers of Congress have passed a resolution directing the President to remove U.S. forces from hostilities — this one with Iran. The Senate vote was 50–48. It was the tenth attempt. It was, by every account, a stunning turnaround. It is also, by every account including its own, largely symbolic and may carry the legal force of a firmly-folded paper boat.

Four Republicans crossed over: Cassidy, Murkowski, Collins, and Paul. One Democrat, Fetterman, voted against. Two Republicans, McConnell and McCormick, did not vote at all — and had they shown up and voted no, the whole thing would have tied 50–50 and sunk. The resolution thus passed because two of its likely opponents were elsewhere, which is the most honest thing that has happened in the Capitol all year.

Senator Cassidy explained his vote by saying the administration had never actually told Americans what was going on, and that he would keep voting for war powers until somebody briefed him. This is a man asking, repeatedly and on the record, to please be told what the war is. He has not been told. He keeps voting. The war keeps going. The briefing remains, like the gash, pending.

The President responded by calling the four crossover Republicans “Losers” who had “made my job more difficult,” and described the resolution as “poorly timed and meaningless.” The resolution, for its part, was introduced as a concurrent resolution, meaning it does not go to the President's desk for a signature, meaning he cannot veto it, meaning everyone is now yelling about a document that has no inbox.

Our War Powers Desk, paddling its tiny boat across the Reflecting Pool, reports that the resolution floats beautifully, directs the current admirably, and changes the current not at all.

It passed because two senators who'd have killed it weren't there. It directs the President to do a thing. The thing is optional. Welcome to war powers in symbolic size.

THE HOSTAGE HOUSEBOAT

In a near-miracle, both chambers of Congress passed the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act — 85–5 in the Senate, 358–32 in the House — the most significant housing legislation since 1990. It speeds construction and, notably, limits big institutional investors from buying up single-family homes. It was a rare, gleaming, fully-bipartisan thing, bobbing in the pool like a houseboat that actually works.

Two hours before the scheduled signing ceremony at the Capitol, the President canceled it. He will not sign the housing bill, he announced, until Congress passes the separate SAVE America Act, a proof-of-citizenship voter-ID measure he has declared a “National Emergency.” In our cover illustration this is depicted accurately: the houseboat is labeled HOUSING BILL and chained to a buoy labeled SAVE ACT FIRST. We changed nothing. That's the news.

Here is the part that makes the hostage situation especially philosophical: under the Constitution, a bill that has passed both chambers becomes law in ten days whether the President signs it or not, as long as Congress stays in session — and this one passed with veto-proof majorities besides. The President is, in effect, refusing to blow out a candle that is going out on its own, while announcing that the candle is his hostage.

Senator Warren, a lead author, noted that huge bipartisan majorities passed a bill to lower housing costs and that the President is refusing, at the eleventh hour, to sign it. Speaker Johnson supported the refusal while also confirming the President will probably sign it within ten days anyway, which is the political equivalent of standing very firm in a doorway that has no door.

INSANE attempted to interview the houseboat. It declined, citing the chain.

It becomes law in ten days whether he signs it or not. He has taken hostage a bill that escapes on its own. This is the strongest possible position to negotiate from, if your goal is a press release.

THE $87.6 BILLION SANDBAG

Into the Reflecting Pool, with a splash heard across the Mall, the White House has lobbed a request for $87.6 billion in supplemental funding. The bag is itemized on the side like a heroic grocery receipt: roughly $67.15 billion for military needs tied largely to the Iran war, plus money for the Ebola response, plus $11.1 billion for farmers, all bundled together so that voting against the war means voting against the farmers and also, somehow, against not getting Ebola.

This is a classic of the genre: the omnibus splash. You cannot remove the war money without getting Ebola on your hands and dropping the farmers in the pool. The bag is designed to be un-untieable. Our Splash Correspondent attempted to untie it and is now, himself, partially Ebola response.

The timing is exquisite. Days after the Senate voted 50–48 that the war should stop, the administration has asked Congress to please fund the war it just voted to stop, which Congress will likely do, because the alternative is explaining to a farmer why his $11.1 billion was attached to a war the funder of which has been directed, symbolically, to end.

The sandbag now sits in the shallow end, leaking $50,000 of “blame management” into the surrounding algae. The ducklings have unionized.

You cannot vote against the war money without dropping the farmers in the pool. That is not a bug in the sandbag. That is the sandbag.

PUMP BUOYS & THE GREAT OIL PROBE

Crude prices dropped. The price at the pump did not drop as fast. In response, the President ordered the Department of Justice to take a look at the oil companies — later naming Exxon and Chevron specifically — over allegations that they are not lowering pump prices quickly enough to suit the moment.

On our cover, the gas pumps bob in the Reflecting Pool like buoys, which is the most accurate possible depiction of American energy policy: floating, bobbing, technically present, going wherever the current goes, dispensing fuel into an algae bloom.

The oil companies, for their part, note that they are large, that prices are complicated, and that crude does not become a gallon of regular unleaded merely because a President has gestured at it on a Tuesday. The DOJ noted that it would “take a look,” which is what the DOJ says about everything, and then took a look, in the way that one looks at a pool one has been told contains a 350-foot gash one cannot see.

INSANE's economists confirm prices are sticky on the way down. This is called “rockets and feathers”: prices rise like rockets and fall like feathers. The feathers, in this case, are floating in the pool, next to the pumps, next to the ducklings, next to the algae, which is winning.

Crude fell like a rocket. The pump price is falling like a feather. The feather is in the pool. Everything is in the pool.

THE MAMDANI SUBWAY CAR DERAILS THE ESTABLISHMENT

Up from New York, crashing into the corner of our cover like a runaway train, comes the night the Democratic establishment got derailed. Three congressional candidates endorsed by Mayor Zohran Mamdani won their primaries on Tuesday — and two of them did it by ousting sitting incumbents.

Former City Comptroller Brad Lander defeated Rep. Dan Goldman handily, around 66% to 34%, in the 10th District. Community organizer Darializa Avila Chevalier edged out five-term Rep. Adriano Espaillat by roughly three points in the 13th — one of the biggest New York House upsets since a young bartender named Ocasio-Cortez beat Joe Crowley back in 2018. And State Assemblymember Claire Valdez took the open 7th District seat outright.

House Democratic Leader Hakeem Jeffries campaigned aggressively against the Mamdani slate. The Mamdani slate won anyway. Mamdani called the trio “our team,” having filmed an ad with them that aired after Game 1 of the NBA Finals in which he passed a basketball back and forth with his three future congressmen. This is, we note, a more functional handoff than anything happening at the Reflecting Pool.

The subway car on our cover bears the slogan MAMDANI EFFECT and the words INCUMBENTS OUT. We did not editorialize. We drew the train. The train did the rest. The train always does.

Jeffries campaigned against them. The train ran anyway. The train does not check the schedule with leadership.
